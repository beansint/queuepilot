Five upgrades

Same loop, five upgrades bolted on

Each one is a small, nameable idea. Together they turn a retrieve-and-generate pipeline into a copilot that knows how much to trust itself.

1

Hybrid retrieval

Embeddings match by meaning — they can miss exact terms like a law number, an error code, or a product name. So QueuePilot adds an old-school keyword matcher (BM25) alongside the dense matcher and blends the two.

Gotcha: the two scores live on different scales, so you normalize before blending — otherwise one drowns out the other. A dial called alpha controls the mix (1.0 = pure meaning, 0.0 = pure keywords).

4

Calibrated confidence

THE BIG ONE

The trap: ask an LLM "how sure are you?" and it says "very!" almost every time — right or wrong. LLMs are overconfident and badly calibrated, so self-reported confidence is noise.

The fix: never ask the model. Build trust from things you can observe — neighbour agreement, retrieval closeness, cross-check consistency, a penalty for missing info.

confidence = f(agreement, closeness, consistency, missing) — never f(LLM's own claim) Play with the real formula
5

Guarded routing + explainability

LangSmith

With a real confidence number, the last node routes instead of always answering: low trust → escalate to a human, missing info → clarify, high trust → answer (grounded in retrieved tickets, fewer hallucinations).

Explainability: --explain itemizes the formula and the per-node trace. Tracing is a flight recorder — you can't unit-test an LLM (same input, different output), so every run is recorded and evaluated in aggregate.

The four signals, in plain words

The confidence score blends four measurable signals. Here's what each one means, with a concrete example — so the sliders and toggles below make sense before you touch them.

Neighbour agreement ×0.5
Of the similar past tickets we retrieved, what fraction share the same winning label? When past cases speak with one voice, that consensus is evidence — so this is the biggest weight.
e.g.4 of the 5 nearest tickets are "Technical Support" → agreement 0.8. Comes from majority_vote.
maps to the "Neighbour agreement" slider
Retrieval closeness ×0.3
How strong is the single best match? The raw top_score is squashed to 0–1 with a sigmoid (an S-shaped curve that gently maps any number into a 0–1 range) before it's blended in.
e.g.a 0.85 top hit means we've genuinely seen this before; a 0.30 top hit means we're guessing.
maps to the "Top match closeness" slider
Cross-check consistency +0.2
Does the LLM's own classification agree with the retrieval majority? Two independent methods pointing the same way earns a bonus; disagreement is a quiet warning sign.
e.g.LLM says "Technical Support" AND the neighbours' majority is "Technical Support" → +0.2; if they disagree, no bonus.
maps to the "LLM agrees with majority" toggle
Missing-info penalty −0.15
Did the assess_missing step find important details absent? We should be less sure when the ticket is under-specified.
e.g.no VPN client version, no error message → subtract 0.15.
maps to the "Key details missing" toggle

Takeaway

Same loop, one extra decision

Classic RAG retrieves and generates; a guarded agentic copilot also decides how much to trust itself (from measured signals, not the model's word) and what to do about it (answer, ask, or escalate) — and can show its work. Internalising calibration alone puts you ahead of most people entering AI/ML.

Self-quiz — 3 questions
1. Why don't we use the LLM's self-reported confidence, and what do we use instead?
LLMs are miscalibrated (systematically overconfident), so self-confidence doesn't track correctness. We blend observable signals — neighbour agreement, retrieval closeness, LLM-vs-majority consistency, minus a missing-info penalty — into a score that does.
2. What breaks if you blend dense + sparse retrieval scores without normalizing first?
The larger-magnitude score dominates, so alpha no longer controls the dense/sparse trade-off; you effectively get single-mode retrieval and lose the hybrid benefit.
3. Why is "escalate under uncertainty" a feature, not a limitation?
A copilot that hands off when genuinely unsure (or when SLA risk is extreme) avoids confident-but-wrong actions on real operations. Trustworthy behaviour under uncertainty is worth more than always answering.
